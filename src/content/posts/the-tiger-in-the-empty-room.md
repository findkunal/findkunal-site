## The sentence that wouldn't leave

I heard it in one of his talks, and it lodged somewhere I couldn't reach to pull it out.

The source of most of our misery is the gap between what *is* and what *should be*.

Simple enough to nod at. Most good ideas are. But I kept turning it over, and the more I looked, the less it behaved like a tidy line and the more it behaved like a crack running through everything I thought I understood about my own head.

This post is my attempt to write down what I found in that crack. The thinking is his. The mistakes are mine.

## The gap is the thing

Look at almost any kind of suffering and you'll find the same shape underneath.

Disappointment is just the distance between what happened and what should have. Resentment is "I should have been treated better." Anxiety is "things should be certain." Even grief carries a quiet "they should still be here." The mind builds an ideal, sets it next to the actual, and then suffers the difference. We call the suffering life.

What Krishnamurti pointed at wasn't "so close the gap, reach the ideal." It was stranger and more uncomfortable than that. He said the gap itself is the invention. Chasing "what should be" is the very thing that stops you from looking at what is. You split yourself in two, the one who is and the one who should be, and you set half of you running after the other half forever. That running has a name. We'll get to it.

## Fear is made of time

Here's the part that genuinely moved something for me.

He said psychological fear is almost never about the present moment. In the actual instant of danger there's no fear. There's just response, action, the body doing what it does. Fear shows up when thought takes the present and throws it forward or back. I felt pain before, it might come again. I have this now, I might lose it. I am this, I might fail to become that.

So fear isn't a reaction to what's here. It's thought leaving what's here and travelling into a past or a future that exists only in the mind. And that travelling is what he meant by psychological time. Not clock time, but the mind's restless motion away from the actual.

Which loops straight back to the gap. The "should," the ideal, the someday-better-me, that's the future the mind keeps inventing, and it runs on the same engine. To want to become something is to live in the gap, and the gap is where fear breeds.

He took it one step further, and this one stung. Thought itself is the source of the fear. The very tool we reach for to solve fear is the thing making it. You can't think your way out, because thinking is the fox you've put in charge of the henhouse.

## The mechanism gone bad

Now the obvious objection, the one my programmer brain threw up straight away. Hang on. Our ancestors survived precisely because experience guided them. You touch fire, you learn, you don't touch it again. Throw memory away and you die.

True. And he never denied it. This is where people misread him into sounding like a fool, and he wasn't one.

He drew a line. In the field of the known, the world of skill and knowledge and physical survival, memory is essential. The surgeon needs her experience. The mechanic needs his. Which berries killed your cousin is information worth keeping. Use it. Lean on it fully.

His knife came down on one specific move. Taking that same mechanism and pointing it at the inner life. At the self, at fear, at how you meet the person in front of you right now. There, memory stops protecting you and starts blinding you.

The ancestor who learned to avoid the tiger was using memory perfectly. The man who can't stop being afraid years after the tiger is gone, who lies awake rehearsing tigers in an empty room, that's the same mechanism gone bad. Same tool. No survival value. Just suffering, made at home.

## How to tell which is which

So which experiences do I trust, and which do I put down? This is the practical knot, and it took me a while to untie. The catch is that "experience" is doing two completely different jobs, and they wear the same clothes.

The test isn't the situation. It's what the memory is reaching toward.

Used well, experience answers a *how* question about the world. Used badly, it answers a *who am I* or *what will become of me* question about the self. Same memory, two destinations.

Say a certain kind of boss burned you once, and now another one like him offers you a job. Watch the two strands pull apart.

One says: last time the contract was vague and I got used, so this time I'll get the terms in writing and check how they treat the people already there. That's the surgeon's memory. Specific. Points at an action. Checkable against new facts. And, most importantly, open. If the contract turns out solid, the caution dissolves. Keep that strand. Act on it.

The other says: bosses like this always betray you. I'll be humiliated again. I'm the kind of person this happens to. Notice what changed. It went vague and total. It's about my identity and my future feeling, not a fact about the world. And it's closed. No contract on earth can disprove "I'm the kind of person this happens to," because that was never a claim about reality. It's the ego protecting an image of itself. That strand doesn't keep me safe. It decides the ending before I've looked, and then quietly arranges for me to be right.

A few tells I now keep in my pocket.

Does the memory point at an action, or hand down a verdict? "Check the contract" is an action. "People like this can't be trusted" is a verdict. Keep actions. Be suspicious of verdicts.

Is it specific and checkable, or total and self-sealing? "The brakes failed on wet roads" is useful. "Everything always falls apart for me" can never be proven wrong, which is exactly the tell that it's a story, not information.

Does it leave me freer to look, or does it arrive already loaded with a feeling that's colouring the thing before I've even seen it? Information clears your sight. Image replaces it.

The burn taught you about fire. It did not earn the right to tell you about tomorrow.

## Where this gets personal: the people we love

Relationships are where this bites hardest, because you can watch it ruin things in real time.

Here's the uncomfortable bit. You don't actually live with your partner. You live with your stored image of them, the running ledger of every past disappointment, every "he always," every "she never." And the cruelty is that the more history you share, the thicker the image gets, and the less you actually see the person standing in the kitchen. You respond to the file, not the human. Two people, each reacting to their saved recording of the other, two ghosts bumping in the dark. That, I think, is why a long relationship can feel so deeply known and so strangely lonely at the same time. You know the image by heart. You stopped seeing the person years ago.

The same test saves you here. "She gets overwhelmed when the week's unplanned, so let's sort Sunday out together" is specific, warm, open, checkable. That's intimacy. Keep it. "He's selfish, that's just who he is" is total, closed, self-sealing. That's a verdict, and once you're holding it you'll read selfishness into a neutral act and miss the moment he actually showed up. The verdict doesn't describe the relationship. It builds it. You get the partner you've already decided you have.

And there's a softer flip side. Drop the image, even for one moment, and you let the person change. The image freezes them as the same careless, distant, difficult person they were, and people tend to become exactly what they're constantly seen as. Seeing someone fresh is one of the few real gifts on offer. It gives them room to be different from your memory of them. That, he'd say, is closer to love than all the accumulated knowing. Love isn't the depth of the file. It's freedom from it.

One guardrail, because I refuse to be naive about this. "Meet them fresh" is never a licence to keep excusing someone who is genuinely, repeatedly harming you. Sometimes the pattern is the fact. The test still holds. Is the memory handing you a checkable truth about how they actually treat you, or a story protecting your ego? Real harm, clearly seen, is information. Act on it. The image to put down is the one distorting your sight, not the one finally letting you see straight.

## The guiding principle that refuses to be one

I wanted to end this with a clean rule to live by. A maxim. Something to screenshot.

And then I caught the trap. A guiding principle is itself a "should." A standard you carry into tomorrow to measure yourself against is the exact machinery we've been pulling apart, thought building the gap again, now dressed up as self-improvement. Hand yourself a crisp rule and you've smuggled the whole disease back in through the front door.

So let it not be a principle. Let it be one move, returned to, with nothing kept between returns.

When something disturbs you, look at what is actually here, before the mind tells you what should be instead.

That's all. Not "stay present" as a target to hit, because then you've got a present-self failing and a future-self winning, and you're back in time. Just the noticing. Done the instant it's needed, dropped the instant it's done.

A few things keep it honest. It isn't a 6 a.m. practice with a box to tick. It only fires when life actually snags you. That snag is the bell. You'll forget constantly, and the forgetting isn't failure. Treating it as failure is just the "should" sneaking back in. No keeping score. Each time is the first time. And the moment you catch yourself thinking *I'm getting good at this*, that's thought congratulating itself, rebuilding the gap out of your own progress. See that too, and let it go.

What's left, once you strip away the carrying-forward, is almost embarrassingly simple. Meet what's in front of you as it is. Not as a discipline. As the only place anything real has ever happened.

## For Jiddu Krishnamurti

None of this is mine. The seeing, if there was any, I had to do myself. He was relentless that agreement is worthless, that a borrowed insight is just another way of dodging the thing. But the map I was reading from was his, drawn over decades of refusing to let his own work harden into a method, because a method is just one more "should."

So this one is for him. With the small, fitting irony that the best way to honour the man is probably to stop quoting him and go look for myself.
